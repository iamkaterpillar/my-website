Using Claude CoWork to grow my LinkedIn network

LinkedIn networking is one of those things I know I should be doing more of but never quite get around to. The mental overhead is real: search for the right person, check if they're a good fit, write something that doesn't sound like a template, track who you've already reached out to. By the time you've done all that for ten people, an hour is gone.

So when I realised Claude CoWork could potentially take on some of that, I wanted to test it.

The setup

CoWork's LinkedIn capabilities aren't built-in by default — you need two things. First, the Claude Desktop app. Second, the Claude in Chrome extension, which is free for paid users. The extension is what gives Claude actual browser control: it can see your LinkedIn session, read pages, click buttons, and type into fields. Combined with the CoWork agent, you've got something that can navigate LinkedIn on your behalf while you watch.

It only works while you're logged in and your browser is open. Claude isn't running anything in the background without you. Every action that sends or connects still goes through you — you're watching it happen in real time.

What I tried first

I started with something immediately useful for work. I wanted to connect with reporters covering crypto and tokenised assets — journalists at places like CoinDesk and Cointelegraph who I should probably know but don't. I had a rough list. I dropped it into Claude and told it what I wanted.

What happened next was kind of wild.

Claude didn't fire off generic connection requests. It searched LinkedIn for each person on the list, verified their profile, and drafted a personalised note based on their role and beat. For Oliver Knight (Deputy Managing Editor at CoinDesk), it wrote something specific to his coverage — not a template, an actual contextual message. It worked through the list one by one, checking each person off as it went.

Some got personalised notes. Others got a plain connection request where I'd already hit my limit for notes that month. It handled both cases gracefully, adapting without me having to intervene.

The reality check

I hit LinkedIn's rate limit for searches partway through the list. Annoying, but not surprising — LinkedIn actively fights automation, and there are guardrails. If you're planning to work through a big list, expect to do it in sessions rather than one go.

The personalised invite limit (200 per month for free accounts) also came up fast once Claude started drafting notes for everyone. Worth budgeting for if you're planning to do this at scale.

What I want to do next

This was just one use case. There are a few more I want to explore:

Drafting replies to messages. I'm bad at keeping up with LinkedIn DMs — not because I don't want to respond, but because writing a thoughtful reply takes time I don't always have. I want to see if Claude can draft responses for me to review and send. The tricky part will be setting guidelines so it sounds like me and not like a bot.

Finding and adding relevant people proactively. Rather than giving it a pre-made list, I want to try telling it what kind of person I want to connect with and let it do the searching. Something like: "Find me BD leads at tier-1 crypto funds who post about RWAs." That's a bit more open-ended, but if it works, it removes the whole first step of the process.

Posting more consistently. This one is probably the highest-leverage thing I could do for my personal brand and it's also the one I procrastinate on the most. I want to explore whether Claude can help me turn notes and ideas into ready-to-post content — not ghostwriting, just removing the friction between having a thought and actually sharing it.

Early days

I've barely scratched the surface here. But even this first experiment made me realise how much time I've been leaving on the table. The work of networking — searching, personalising, following up — is exactly the kind of repetitive, contextual task that AI handles well.

The relationship part is still mine. The admin? Happy to delegate.
